Description
PHY251 introduces the transition of physics from classical (Newton) mechanics to relativistic mechanics and quantum mechanics, happening during the first half of the 20th century (1904-1940). The story begins with the so-called two “dark clouds” (identified by Lord Kelvin) in classical physics: the Michelson–Morley experiment, and the black body radiation. These two problems which baffled classical mechanics eventually led to special relativity and quantum mechanics, which completely changed the view of classical physics, and serve as foundation of our contemporary view of the universe.
The course will briefly follow the history of this transition and introduce the basics of special relativity and quantum mechanics. For special relativity we will learn about the core concept of the invariance of the speed of light. We will study the consequence of this concept, and its mathematic description of Lorentz transformation. Then we will move to the quantum nature of light and matters, and study the De Broglie wave and Schrodinger equation. We will learn a few important examples of the quantum mechanical world, including potential wells and potential walls, and hydrogen atoms and atomic structures.
